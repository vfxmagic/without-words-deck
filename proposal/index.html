What we see

A contained show where the effects should be felt, never seen.

Without Words is character-led and tightly held — a church, a high school, a youth group, ten years of a ministry distilled into a season. The right VFX instinct here is restraint: the work should disappear into the story, not announce itself. The bulk of it is clear and well within our lane — the texting that runs through every episode, the invisible craft around the performances (the harness on the crucifixion, the blood on the white face-paint, the screens), and a clean season-wide finish.

Two creative decisions sit on top of that and shape everything else. We've already mapped them — so that when a director boards, you're choosing from a clear menu instead of discovering surprises in post. That's the whole job of good VFX planning: make the expensive decisions early, while they're still cheap.

Our approach to your show

Where the work — and the money — actually lives.

The texting
A production decision, not just post
You raised the real fork yourself — texts on the phone screen versus on the viewer's screen (your Mom's Night Out reference). The catch most shows miss: the on-screen version needs clean shots of the phones grabbed on the day. We'd lock one approach for the whole series before the camera rolls — so it's a style choice, never a costly fix later.
The performances
The one big lever — your choice, up front
The gospel scenes can be shot as a grounded, filmed stage performance, or lifted with light, atmosphere and a sense of the supernatural. That single decision is the largest swing in the budget by a wide margin. We present it as a clear choice now, with you in control — not a number that quietly balloons once the look is set.
The invisible work
Seams the audience never sees
The crucifixion harness, the stone-and-blood moment, the white-face makeup holding across long performance days, the projected verses and screens — handled so they read as real and never pull a viewer out of the moment. This is the quiet craft that a faith audience feels even when they can't name it.
How the budget works

Built the way you described — full and modular, then trimmed to land it.

Our job is to make sure every dollar lands where it shows — on screen. You said you want to build a budget where money's no object first, then cut from there. That's exactly how we'd structure it: an honest, complete picture, broken into independent pieces, so every element can be scaled or removed without unpicking the rest. You raise against the full number, then we trim together to land the show.

The actual figures wait on two answers — the texting method, and whether the performances are grounded or lifted — plus a director aboard to lock the scripts. A budget set before those is a budget you'd revise. Thirty minutes on a call and we'll hand you one that holds.
How we'd structure the options

Three packages — you choose how far the look goes.

Rather than one figure, we'd build it as three modular tiers, so you can match the spend to the show you're making — and to what you raise. Each tier contains the one before it.

Foundation Invisible & clean

Screen inserts, the texting and device work, and clean-up. Everything the scripts need as written, finished so it disappears into the story. Our recommended starting point.

Enhanced Lifted moments

Foundation, plus performance enhancement, atmospherics, and a handful of signature moments where the gospel beats earn a little more.

Premium Full stylised

The fully heightened, cinematic treatment of the performances — the supernatural, divine-look version, end to end, with its own design stage.

Our steer is to start at Foundation and lift only the moments that earn it — restraint usually serves this material better, and it keeps the budget honest. But it's your show; we'll build whichever tier you're making.
